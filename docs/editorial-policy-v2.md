# Waves Editorial Policy — v2

---

## §5.1 Our editorial mission

Waves publishes educational content because our customers deserve better than a sales funnel dressed as a blog. If you're trying to figure out what's eating your St. Augustine, or whether your termite bond is actually worth renewing, you shouldn't have to wade through three affiliate pitches to get to a straight answer.

Our mission is simple: write the kind of content we'd want to read if we were the customer. That means specificity over generality — "Southern chinch bugs feed at the base of the blade, so yellowing starts at ground level" beats "chinch bugs damage lawns." It means being honest about when a problem is small enough to DIY and when it really isn't. And it means staying local — the pest pressure, lawn types, and weather patterns in Bradenton are not the ones in Phoenix or Pittsburgh, and generic advice will steer you wrong.

We'd rather lose a sale by telling you your grass just needs water than win one by selling you a treatment you don't need.

## §5.2 Who writes and reviews our content

Every post on this site goes through a defined workflow before it publishes, and the people in that workflow are real humans with real accountability — not anonymous "our editorial team."

**Authors.** The person who writes the first draft. Usually a Waves technician writing from field experience; sometimes a broader Waves contributor when the topic is company-level (pricing, service areas, company policy).

**Technical reviewers.** Named humans with current FDACS licensing. They review posts that touch pest biology, pesticide use, treatment protocols, or lawn pathology. If a post says "apply bifenthrin at the labeled rate," the reviewer confirms the label, the rate, and the legal context under Florida Administrative Code 5E-14. Their name and license number appear on the post.

**Fact-checkers.** Separate humans checking claims that don't require a license — pricing, service areas, dates, named neighborhoods, sourcing. The fact-checker's name is on every post.

We run two review tracks depending on what a post claims:

- **Technical track.** Posts making claims about pests, pesticides, treatment methods, application rates, safety, or lawn pathology go through FDACS-licensed technical review *and* fact-check before publishing. A post describing how we treat German roaches, or recommending a specific product for chinch bugs, lives on this track.
- **Editorial track.** Posts about pricing, service areas, company policy, general lawn care practices (watering, mowing heights, seasonal scheduling), or company news go through fact-check only. A post explaining our pricing tiers or describing our service footprint lives on this track.

A lawn-watering tip doesn't need a pesticide license to approve. A pesticide recommendation does. The two-track system keeps licensed review where it actually matters, which is what makes it sustainable.

You can see each person's background at their author page. We list years in SWFL pest control, specialties, and license numbers where applicable. If you ever want to know who signed off on a claim, it's one click away.

## §5.3 Our fact-checking process

**Before a post publishes:**

1. **Track assignment.** The post is assigned to the technical track or editorial track based on what it claims. Posts that mix technical and editorial claims default to the technical track.
2. **Technical review** (technical track only). An FDACS-licensed reviewer reads the draft against original sources. Anything about pest identification, pesticide use, life cycles, application rates, or lawn pathology has to match UF/IFAS research, FDACS rulings, or documented field observations. If it doesn't, the claim gets cut or rewritten.
3. **Fact-check** (both tracks). A reviewer checks everything else — pricing figures against our current pricing engine, service areas against our active license footprint, dates against calendar reality, named neighborhoods against maps. We do not copy pricing from competitor sites; every dollar figure traces back to Waves' own quoted work.

**After a post publishes:**

- **Scheduled review.** Every post has a review cadence set at publish time. When the cadence fires, the post goes back through review with a fresh eye on whether anything has changed: new products, revised regulations, updated Waves pricing.
- **Reader-triggered review.** If a reader tells us a post wasn't helpful, that post drops into our audit queue regardless of where it is in its scheduled cadence. A downvote doesn't change the post on its own — but it does push a human to look at it again.

We date every checkpoint. **Published** is when the post first went live. **Updated** is the last time a human made a change. **Technically reviewed** is the last time a licensed human verified the technical claims. **Fact-checked** is the last time a human verified the non-technical claims. Four dates, all visible on every post.

## §5.4 How we source data and claims

Where our claims come from, in order of how much weight each one gets:

- **Peer-reviewed and extension research.** UF/IFAS extension publications are our default source for pest biology and lawn science. They're written by people who work in Florida, studying Florida pests, and they're updated when the research changes. FDACS publications cover the regulatory and licensing side.
- **Regulatory sources.** Florida Administrative Code chapter 5E-14 governs pesticide application. Florida Statutes chapter 482 governs structural pest control operators. We cite these directly when a post describes what a licensed operator can or can't legally do.
- **Waves' pricing engine.** Anything that names a dollar figure — "a one-time German roach treatment typically runs $X–Y for a 2,000 sqft home" — comes from our own pricing engine and our own book of work, not from competitor sites or industry averages. Pricing posts carry a pricing-transparency disclosure noting the source.
- **Field observations.** Our technicians document what they find on service calls: which pests are active in which months, what's working, what isn't. These observations inform seasonal content but never stand alone; we pair them with extension research or note them as field observations from our service work.

When we can't point to a source for a claim, we cut the claim.

## §5.5 Correction policy

If we got something wrong, we want to know.

**Reporting a correction.** Email contact@wavespestcontrol.com with the URL of the post and what you think is wrong. Include your source if you have one. We read every message.

**How we handle corrections:**

- If you're right and the fix is small (a typo, a date, a clarifying word), we update the post and bump the "updated" date. No visible note — the change speaks for itself.
- If you're right and the fix is material — a corrected pricing figure, a changed recommendation, a retracted claim — we update the post AND add a visible "Corrected on YYYY-MM-DD" note at the top of the page describing what changed and why. The old claim is not silently overwritten.
- If we disagree, we respond explaining why, and we keep the post as-is. We'll still tell you what we found.

**Response times:**

- Standard corrections: we respond within **five business days**.
- Harmful corrections — wrong pesticide concentration, wrong PPE recommendation, wrong timing for a weather-sensitive treatment — we **acknowledge within 4 hours** and fix **within 24 hours**.

We do not silently edit claims. If we got it wrong, we note that we got it wrong.

## §5.6 Review cadence

Posts don't stay accurate on their own. Every post on this site carries a review cadence, set at publish time, that schedules a human to look at it again on a regular interval.

- **Monthly — Current-month seasonal content.** The live active-month reference post (e.g., "what's active in your SWFL yard this month") gets reviewed every month while it's the current-month post, because it's the one readers are acting on right now.
- **Before each relevant season — Other time-sensitive content.** Seasonal calendars, pre-emergent timing windows, fertilizer blackout dates. These shift with weather and state rulings; we reread before each season they apply to rather than on a forced monthly cadence. Quarterly at minimum.
- **Quarterly — Pricing-sensitive content.** Cost breakdowns, tier comparisons, service-area pricing. Our pricing engine updates when input costs shift, and cost posts follow.
- **Annually — Evergreen content.** Pest biology explainers, diagnostic guides, grass-type care guides. The underlying science doesn't shift often, but we still reread the post every twelve months to catch dated references and confirm nothing's changed.

Reader feedback can override the schedule. If a post accumulates "not helpful" votes, it jumps ahead of its cadence into the audit queue regardless of when it last got reviewed.

## §5.8 AI disclosure

We use AI to help draft content. We're telling you that up front because we think the interesting question isn't "did AI touch this," it's "is it correct."

Every post on this site, regardless of how it was drafted, is read in full by a named human reviewer before publishing — an FDACS-licensed reviewer for posts making pesticide, pest-biology, or safety claims, and a fact-checker for all posts. The reviewer puts their name on the post. If a claim is wrong, the human is accountable for it — not the tool that drafted it.

AI helps us cover more ground, faster. Human expertise is what makes the coverage trustworthy.
